Does wealth affect happiness of people and to which extent?

Finally, in the dashboard above, we present the level of happiness around the world, along with each country's GDP per capita score. The happiness is displayed in the chosen colour, with yellow countries being the happiest and as their rank declines, the colour shifts to purple. A quick overview of the scores shows us that counties with a GDP per capita score above the 1.0 line tend to be happier. Of course, this is not an absolute, as switching through the years we see China's GDP per capita score rising, but their happiness rank still remains in the purple colours: never truly surpassing the 79th position and usually ranking in the 80s and 90s.

However, the relation between GDP and happiness is not a proportional one, as we can see that the top 10 economies per year, the strongest economy doesn't corellate to the happiest country. That can be better observed in the dashboard below, titled 'Top 10 Countries Happiness and their GDP per Capita over the years' where we can see two graphs, with the 10 happiest ranked countries displayed above and their respective GDP displayed below. Finland, which consistently ranks as the happiest country has a GDP per capita score of 1.45 which places her as the 20th wealthiest country for that year. A quick but strong conclusion follows this: after a specific economic threshold is surpassed, wealth is clearly not the only aspect which contributes to a country's happines.
